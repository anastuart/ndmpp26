Wells' Metacognitive Model

Metacognitive therapy (MCT) targets the beliefs people hold about their own thinking — not the content of the thoughts themselves. The model distinguishes between Type 1 worry (about external events) and Type 2 worry (about the worry itself), and focuses on modifying unhelpful thought control strategies and metacognitive beliefs that maintain distress.

Thought Control Strategies
Exploring how the client attempts to manage, suppress, or engage with worry and rumination
01When you were worrying, did you do anything to try to control it? What was that?
02Do you ever try not to think about something in case it triggers worrying?
03When you start worrying, do you need to think it through in order to feel OK?
04When you're in the midst of a worry, have you ever decided just to stop it? What happened?
05What happens when you try to push the thought away — does it come back stronger?
06Is there a particular way you try to manage these thoughts when they arise?

Behavioural Responses
Identifying avoidance, reassurance-seeking, and other behaviours that maintain the worry cycle
01Do you avoid certain situations in order to prevent yourself from worrying?
02Do you rely on other people to stop you worrying — for example, by asking for reassurance?
03Do you avoid or search for information in order to prevent worrying?
04Do you do anything to prevent worry and anxiety from harming you?
05Do you do anything to try and relax when the worry becomes intense?
06Are there things you used to do that you've stopped doing because of your worry?

Socialisation to the Model
Helping the client see that beliefs about worry — not the worry content — drive the problem
01If you thought that worrying was only a good thing — that it helped you survive — how much of a problem would worry be?
02If you knew that worrying could be controlled, how much of a problem would you have with it?
03What do you think would happen if you just stopped worrying altogether?
04Do you think worrying serves a purpose for you? What might that be?
05So it sounds like part of you believes worry is helpful, but another part finds it unbearable — would you say that's accurate?

Challenging Uncontrollability Beliefs
Questioning the belief that worry is uncontrollable through verbal reattribution
01If worrying is uncontrollable, how does it ever stop?
02What happens to your worry if suddenly the phone rings and you have to answer it?
03Have you ever tried to postpone worry until another time? What happened?
04Can you rate how strongly you believe worry is uncontrollable right now — from 0 to 100%?
05Are there times when you've been worrying and something interrupted it — and the worry just stopped?
06If worry were truly uncontrollable, would you ever be able to fall asleep?

Questioning the Evidence
Examining the evidence for danger beliefs about worry — testing whether feared consequences have materialised
01What evidence do you have that worry causes [the feared outcome]?
02How long have you been a worrier? Why hasn't [the feared consequence] happened yet?
03Has worrying ever actually prevented the thing you were worried about from happening?
04If worry really could cause [e.g., a breakdown], wouldn't it have happened by now given how much you worry?
05What's the difference between feeling like something bad will happen and it actually happening?

The Dissonance Technique
Highlighting the contradiction between positive and negative beliefs about worry to weaken both
01On the one hand you believe worry helps you [e.g., be prepared], yet on the other you think it could [e.g., cause a breakdown] — how can both be true?
02If worry is helpful, why does it feel so distressing?
03Could it be that worry isn't as useful as it seems — or perhaps not as harmful?
04You've told me worry keeps you safe, but also that it's ruining your life — which of those feels more true right now?
05It sounds like you're in two minds about worry. What do you make of that conflict?
