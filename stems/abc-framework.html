Antecedent–Behaviour–Consequence (ABC)

Behavioural psychology's ABC framework is useful when a challenging situation is unclear. It draws out the key facts of an event in a structured, linear sequence — making it easier to identify patterns, triggers, and outcomes.

Albert Ellis's Rational Emotive Behaviour Therapy (REBT) extends this with two further dimensions — T for thoughts/cognitions and E for emotions — giving a fuller picture of the internal experience surrounding the event.

ABC + REBT · 5 dimensions
REBT

Albert Ellis's extension: The standard ABC captures observable events. REBT adds the inner world — what the client was thinking (T) and feeling (E) at each stage. Together these reveal not just what happened, but what meaning the client made of it and how that shaped their response.

A
Antecedent
Draw out the linear sequence — what happened, in order, and what led to the behaviour
Purpose: Establish the factual chronology. Build a clear, sequenced timeline before introducing interpretation or emotion.
01Let's go back to the beginning — what was the very first thing that happened?
02Walk me through it step by step — what happened first? And then what?
03What was going on just before this happened — what was the situation or context?
04Was there anything that seemed to set it off or lead up to it?
05What was the result of that — what happened next after x?
06So just to check the sequence — first x, then y, then z? Is that right?

B
Behaviour
Focus on observable, concrete actions — what was actually done or said
Purpose: Separate observable behaviour from interpretation. You want specifics — words said, actions taken, what was visible externally.
01What did you actually do in that moment — concretely, what were your actions?
02What did you say — can you remember the actual words?
03What did the other person do? What were their specific actions?
04What did he or she say — as specifically as you can remember?
05If someone had been watching this on camera — what would they have seen you do?
06What did you do that you wish you hadn't — or not do that you wish you had?

C
Consequence
Help the client see the result — what followed, for them and for others
Purpose: Examine outcomes to build awareness of patterns and cause-effect relationships.
01What happened afterward — immediately after that?
02What did you do after — how did you respond to what had happened?
03What did the other person do or say after that?
04How did things unfold from there — what was the overall outcome?
05What was the effect of that — on you, on the other person, on the relationship?
06Looking back — what do you think the consequence of that behaviour was, even if it wasn't immediate?

T
Thoughts — REBT extension
Surface the automatic cognitions — what the client was telling themselves
Purpose: Uncover the interpretive layer. What meaning did the client make of the event? These automatic thoughts often drive the emotional and behavioural response more than the event itself.
01What was going through your mind at that point — what were you thinking?
02What did you tell yourself about what was happening?
03What was the thought — the automatic one — that came first?
04What did you assume the other person was thinking or intending?
05What did you make it mean — about you, about them, about the situation?
06Looking back, was there another way to read that situation — even if it didn't occur to you at the time?

E
Emotions — REBT extension
Map the emotional landscape — before, during, after, and for the other person
Purpose: Track how emotion shifted across the event. Asking across time points reveals where the most intense distress actually sits — which is often not where the client first assumes.
01How were you feeling just before it happened — what was your emotional state going in?
02What emotions came up during the situation — as it was unfolding?
03How did you feel right afterward — in those first moments?
04And later — when you had time to reflect — what feelings were there?
05How do you think the other person felt — before, during, and after?
06If you think about the whole situation now — what's the feeling that stays with you most?
