The 5 Ps Framework

The 5 Ps is a widely used clinical formulation framework that organises information into five interconnected domains. It moves beyond description of symptoms to ask why this person, why this problem, why now — building a shared understanding of how difficulties developed and what keeps them going, while also identifying strengths and resources.

Clinical Formulation · Macneil et al. (2012)
Presenting Problem
Establishing what the client is experiencing now — the nature, severity, and scope of current difficulties
01What has brought you in to see me today?
02Can you describe what you've been experiencing — in your own words?
03What is the main difficulty you'd like help with?
04How long has this been going on?
05How would you describe the severity — how much is this affecting your day-to-day life?
06Can you walk me through what a typical day looks like for you at the moment?
07Are there other difficulties going on alongside this, or is this the main thing?
08How are things at work, at home, and in your relationships right now?

Predisposing Factors
Exploring background vulnerabilities — early experiences, developmental history, temperament, and biological factors that made the person more susceptible
01Can you tell me a bit about your background — what was life like growing up?
02What was your family environment like when you were young?
03Were there any significant events or experiences in your childhood that still stand out to you?
04Would you say you've always been someone who tends toward [anxiety, low mood, worry], or is this more recent?
05Is there any history of mental health difficulties in your family?
06How would you describe yourself as a child — were you confident, anxious, shy, outgoing?
07Were there any experiences early on that might have shaped how you see yourself or other people?
08What messages did you pick up growing up — about yourself, about the world, about what was expected of you?
09Have you experienced any trauma, abuse, neglect, or significant loss in your life?
10What was school like for you — socially and academically?

Precipitating Factors
Identifying the triggers and life events that brought on the current episode — what happened to activate existing vulnerabilities
01What was happening in your life around the time this started?
02Can you think of a particular event or change that seemed to set things off?
03Was there anything stressful going on — at work, at home, in your relationships — when you first noticed things getting worse?
04Had there been any major changes or transitions in your life around that time?
05Was there a loss, a conflict, or a disappointment that came just before this?
06What made you decide to seek help now, rather than earlier?
07Looking back, was there a moment when things shifted from manageable to overwhelming?
08Did anything happen that reminded you of earlier experiences — something that felt familiar?

Perpetuating Factors
Understanding what keeps the problem going — the thoughts, behaviours, relationships, and circumstances that maintain the cycle
01What do you think keeps this going — what stops it from getting better on its own?
02When you feel [the emotion], what do you tend to do? And does that help in the long run?
03Are there things you avoid because of this? What effect does the avoidance have?
04Are there particular thought patterns or beliefs that seem to keep you stuck?
05How do the people around you respond when you're struggling — and does that response help or make things harder?
06Do you find yourself doing things to cope that give you short-term relief but keep the problem going?
07Are there ongoing stressors — financial, relational, work-related — that make it harder to recover?
08If we drew a cycle of what happens — the trigger, the thought, the feeling, and what you do — what would it look like?
09Have you noticed any patterns — situations where the same thing seems to happen again and again?

Protective Factors
Identifying strengths, resources, coping skills, relationships, and values that buffer against distress and support recovery
01What keeps you going, even on the hardest days?
02Are there people in your life you feel you can turn to for support?
03What strengths or qualities do you have that have helped you get through difficult times before?
04Have you been through something hard in the past and come out the other side? What helped then?
05Are there activities, routines, or interests that give you a sense of purpose or enjoyment?
06What matters most to you in life — what are the things you really value?
07Is there anything that's currently going well for you — even if it feels small?
08What would your best friend or someone who knows you well say your strengths are?
09Are there any cultural, spiritual, or community connections that are important to you?
