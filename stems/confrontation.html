Confrontation in Counselling

Confrontation is not aggressive challenging — it is a gentle attempt to bring awareness to something the client may have overlooked or avoided. The counsellor identifies mixed or incongruent messages, helps the client explore them, and evaluates the resulting change. Discrepancies can exist between thoughts and feelings, thoughts and actions, feelings and actions, or a combination of all three.

Ivey, Ivey & Zalaquett (2018) · CCS
CCS

Client Change Scale: The CCS tracks client movement across five levels — Denial (refuses to acknowledge), Partial Examination (considers but minimises), Acceptance & Recognition (sees the pattern, feels stuck), Creation of a New Solution (generates concrete steps), and Transcendence (integrates change into broader life). Effective confrontation moves the client upward through these levels.

Identifying Discrepancies
Standard formats for reflecting incongruence between the client's words, feelings, and actions
01On the one hand you say [x], but on the other hand [y] — can you help me understand that?
02You say [x], but you do [y] — what do you make of that?
03Your words say [x], but your actions say [y] — does that fit with how you see it?
04It sounds like part of you wants [x], but another part of you is doing [y] — does that resonate?
05You mentioned that [value or goal] is really important to you, and yet it sounds like [behaviour] is getting in the way of that.
06I'm hearing two things — [A] and [B] — and I'm wondering how those fit together for you.
07You're telling me [x], but I noticed that [non-verbal cue or behaviour] — what's going on there?

Deepening Awareness
Following up on the identified discrepancy to help the client explore and understand it
01What do you think is behind that gap between what you want and what you're doing?
02If someone you cared about described this same situation, what would you notice?
03What would need to change for your actions to line up with what you've told me matters most?
04How does it feel to hear that reflected back to you?
05What's stopping you from doing the thing you say you want to do?
06Is there something about [the current behaviour] that's serving you — even if it's not what you ultimately want?

Moving Along the Client Change Scale
Stems calibrated to each CCS level — meeting the client where they are and gently inviting movement
01Denial → I hear you saying this isn't really an issue — and at the same time you've come here today. What brought you in?
02Denial → You're telling me everything is fine, but you also mentioned [x] earlier. Can we sit with that for a moment?
03Partial Examination → It sounds like you're starting to see that there might be something worth looking at here. What's beginning to stand out to you?
04Partial Examination → You've acknowledged this might be a pattern — what would it mean if that were true?
05Acceptance → You seem to be recognising that this is a real pattern — what do you think could be done about it?
06Acceptance → Now that you can see it clearly, what feels like the biggest barrier to change?
07New Solution → You've identified the issue — what would a first step toward change look like for you?
08New Solution → What's one small thing you could do this week that would be different from what you usually do?
09Transcendence → It sounds like you've really shifted how you approach this — how does that feel compared to where you started?
10Transcendence → Looking back, what helped you make that shift — and how might you apply that to other areas?
