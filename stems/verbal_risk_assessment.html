Verbal Risk Assessment

A verbal risk assessment gathers information across several domains — suicidal ideation, planning, access to means, previous behaviour, and protective factors — to estimate the client's level of risk and decide on a management plan. It is challenging to communicate with a client in distress, but gathering this information is essential. It is important to be persistent and compassionate throughout.

The assessment should lead to a risk classification (Low, Moderate, or High) and an appropriate response, developed collaboratively with the client where possible.

SAK Suicide Risk Screener
A structured sequence of questions to systematically assess suicide risk — begin by asking permission before proceeding
00Opening: "I need to ask you a few questions on how you have been feeling — is that ok?"
01In the past 6 weeks, did you feel so sad that nothing could cheer you up?
02In the past 6 weeks, how often did you feel like a burden?
03In the past 6 weeks, how often did you feel intense shame or guilt?
04In the past 6 weeks, how often did you feel worthless?
05Have you ever thought about killing yourself? → If yes: How many times have you tried to kill yourself? When was the last attempt?
06Have you gone through any traumatic event recently? (e.g. family breakdown, relationship problem, loss of loved one, child custody issues, unemployment, criminal punishments, financial or legal issues)
07Have things been so bad that you have thought about — or made — any plan for suicide? → If yes: How often do you have thoughts of suicide? How long have you been having these thoughts? How intense are they at their worst?
08Do you have a current plan for how you would attempt suicide? → If yes: What method? Where? Have preparations been made? How likely are you to act on this plan in the near future?
09What has stopped you from acting on these suicidal thoughts?
10Do you have any friends or family members you can confide in if you have a serious problem? → How often are you in contact with them? What has helped you through difficult times in the past?

Presence of a Suicide Plan
Assessing whether the client has a specific plan, including method and frequency of planning thoughts.
01Does the client have a plan?
02Have they specified a method or place?
03How often is the client thinking about their plan?
PlanMethodIntent
!

Plans or preparations for suicide indicate serious suicidal intent. This includes putting affairs in order, saying goodbyes, or giving away possessions. The presence of a specific, well-developed plan significantly elevates risk — escalate accordingly.

Access to Means
Determining whether the client has the means available to carry out a plan, and how lethal those means are.
01Does the client have access to the means (e.g. weapons, medication) to carry out their plan?
02How lethal is the method?
03Does the client have an occupation that may mean they have access to lethal means (e.g. police officer, health worker)?

Risk Level Classification
Indicators and suggested clinical responses for Low, Moderate, and High risk presentations
LOWIndicators: No plans or intent · No prior attempts · Few risk factors · Identifiable protective factors
LOWResponse: Monitor and review risk frequently · Identify supports and provide contact details · Consult with a colleague or supervisor · Refer to safety plan and keep-safe strategies
MODIndicators: Suicidal thoughts of limited frequency, intensity, and duration · No plans or intent · Some risk factors present · Some protective factors
MODResponse: Request permission to organise a specialised mental health assessment as soon as possible · Refer to safety plan · Consult with a colleague or supervisor · Remove means where possible · Review daily
HIGHIndicators: Frequent, intense, enduring suicidal thoughts · Clear intent with specific, well-developed plans · Few or no alternatives considered · Many risk factors · Few or no protective factors (or highly changeable presentation)
HIGHResponse: If immediate intention to act — contact mental health crisis team immediately and do not leave client alone · Remove means where possible · Call ambulance/police if client refuses assessment and crisis team is unavailable · Consult with a colleague or supervisor

Automatic High Risk: If a client presents as agitated, intoxicated, disoriented or confused, engaged in self-harm, delusional, or hallucinating — and is expressing thoughts of suicide — risk level is automatically HIGH, regardless of other indicators.

Suicide Risk Management
Steps to decide, in consultation with the client, how to manage risk and maintain safety following assessment.
01Develop a safety plan with the client.
02Make referrals to other health professionals or services as appropriate.
03Consider contacting the client's family or support network (with consent).
04Arrange a formal risk assessment if indicated.
